Why Aperio

A language for the shape you already think in.

an essay on naming

A programming language is not, mostly, a notation for computation. It is a notation for the shape a system holds in someone's head. Code that compiles is code whose author convinced the compiler that the shape they meant is the shape they typed. The compiler is the strictest reader they will ever have.

Most languages give you primitives — values, functions, types, modules — and ask you to assemble the shape yourself, by convention, in a way the compiler cannot see and therefore cannot enforce. Erlang did something different along one axis: it named the shape of failure flow, and let the runtime enforce it. Almost everything good about Erlang in production is downstream of that one decision. But Erlang only named one axis, and no language since has named the rest.

Aperio names the whole shape — memory regions, typed contracts, cost regimes, audit invariants — and treats them as facets of a single shape rather than four separate features. The shape was always there, in every codebase, in every senior engineer's head; no language so far has declined to launder it through unrelated abstractions. Aperio is what happens when you finally don't.

— the shape, named —

§ I  ·  the shape

Every senior engineer already holds it — an infinite lotus, drillable at will.

Read any production codebase with a senior engineer and notice what they actually do. They do not build, in their head, one diagram. They build several at once, and each one is recursive all the way down. Understanding is the braiding between them and the drilling within them.

There is an import graph — what depends on what, which module knows about which, where the fan-in concentrates and where the fan-out lives. There is a runtime tree — what spawns what, who calls whom at execution time, where data flows, where lifetimes nest, where failure escalates. There is a domain shape — the concepts in the world this code is about: the Orders and Accounts and Events and Strategies that the runtime tree and the import graph are both, each in their own way, trying to honor. Each is hierarchical, with typed surfaces between layers; lateral coordination is either expensive or impossible.

And each is recursive. Drill into any node — this service in the runtime tree — and a lotus waits inside: handlers nested in the service, ops nested in handlers, primitives nested in ops. Drill into a domain concept and the same is true: an Order has line items, line items have prices, prices have currencies, currencies have rounding rules. The shape repeats at every depth, with the same braiding rules every time. What the engineer holds in their head is not finite. It is infinite lotus — recursive at every node, drillable from any angle, with shape waiting wherever they choose to look.

This is why a senior engineer seems to know everything about a system they have worked on long enough. They do not. They hold the recursive structure and the cross-projection correspondences. When asked about some specific piece they instantiate the shape at that depth on demand, and the answer is right because the shape is consistent. They do not look it up. They reconstruct it. The structure of the reconstruction is the lotus.

The act that distinguishes them is the braiding: noticing that this node in the import graph is that concept in the domain, and at runtime that process spins up those handlers — and that the three coincide. The unit of understanding is a node appearing in multiple projections at once with consistent meaning across all of them. The mental model is that braided, recursive structure, and it is itself lotus-shaped: loci nested in loci, drillable from any depth, with each locus identified by appearing in multiple projections at once.

None of this is in the code. It is reconstructed on every onboarding, every code review, every architecture document, every incident retrospective. Most of what review and onboarding actually do is keep the braid intact across people who do not share it. When a teammate writes code that "violates the shape," the violation is almost never a compile error; it is something a human has to catch, in review, before it ships.

The diagram understates the claim twice. First: it is one projection of three. The import graph braids onto it; the domain shape braids onto it; the locus identities at the braid points are the load-bearing thing. Second: it is one altitude of infinite. Drill into app and another lotus waits; drill into one of those nodes, another. The actual mental model has no floor — it bottoms only at primitive operations, far below any whiteboard could draw. Aperio's bet is that when this whole structure — three-projection-braided, recursive without floor — is the syntax, the compiler enforces what every team currently enforces by code review and tribal memory. The braid you would draw on the whiteboard during onboarding becomes the program. So do the other two. So does the recursion they conceal.

§ II  ·  erlang's move, extended

OTP named one slice. There are four.

Erlang chose one facet of the shape — failure flow — and made it syntactic. A process can fail; a supervisor decides what happens next; the rules live in the source, not the runbook. The rest of the shape (memory, typed contracts, cost regimes, structural invariants) Erlang left to convention.

The four facets, each made load-bearing for the compiler in Aperio:

i  ·  lifetime

Region-shaped memory

A locus owns its region. The region's lifetime is the locus's lifetime. Dissolution frees it wholesale. No GC. No borrow checker. The tree provides the ownership; the allocator just respects it.

ii  ·  surface

Typed contracts

Each locus declares what it exposes upward and consumes downward. The contract is the only typed surface between layers. Sibling reach is not a runtime error; it is unsayable.

iii  ·  regime

Projection class

N≈10, N≈300, N≈10 000 are different cost regimes — different allocators, different access patterns, different generated code. Aperio names them rich, chunked, recognition and writes the body once.

iv  ·  audit

Closure invariants

"The books must balance, at this band, at this epoch." Asserted in source; checked by the runtime; if violated, the parent receives a typed ClosureViolation carrying the diff. Books that don't balance are not a panic — they are a structured fact.

Each axis has been studied in isolation — region-based memory management, capability systems, generic programming, design by contract. Aperio's claim is that they are not four features. They are the same shape on four different axes, and naming the shape once gives the compiler all four with one declaration site.

— the shape is not arbitrary —

§ III  ·  the manifold

The same shape appears in every context that processes structure.

The skeptical reading is that the lotus tree is an aesthetic choice. The shape happens to be useful for some kinds of programs. Aperio happens to encode it. Fine, but contingent.

Same shape: a hierarchy of layers, each cheaper and more numerous toward the leaves, each costlier and more authoritative toward the root; a typed surface at each interface; the bulk of motion vertical; lateral coordination either expensive or impossible. The lotus shape is not Aperio's contribution. It is what converged into being long before any of us; Aperio's contribution is the refusal to keep laundering it through unrelated abstractions.

§ IV  ·  three altitudes

Inside any locus is the same shape, one layer down.

Recursion is not a feature added to the shape; it is a property of the shape itself. At every altitude, the same nested rings appear. Zoom into any single ring and you find the same diagram waiting. Loci, like cells, are not made of something else — they are made of smaller loci, all the way down to a primitive operation, which is the only thing that is not itself a locus.

at the company

the whole system org  ·  net  ·  cluster  ·  process

inside a process

a running program app  ·  service  ·  handler  ·  op

inside a function

a single body fn  ·  block  ·  stmt  ·  primitive

Three diagrams; one shape. The framework's recursion gives the hierarchy for free; Aperio's lifecycle methods give the deterministic free-points; the contract block gives the access discipline. Together they give region-based memory without the inference problems that have historically made region-based memory hard — the hierarchy is no longer something the compiler has to discover. It is something the source has already declared.

— what does this look like in code? —

§ V  ·  what aperio expresses trivially

Some programs are a handful of lines in Aperio and hundreds in any mainstream language.

Consider one of the most familiar problems in systems programming: a server that listens on a port, accepts connections, and shuts down cleanly on Ctrl-C. Every connection's resources — its socket, its buffers, its arena — must be released. New connections must be refused during shutdown. In-flight requests must be allowed to finish. The cascade must run from leaves to root, deterministically, without leaks, without deadlock. Every server programmer has lived this code, in every language, more than once.

In Aperio:

aperio

a server with clean shutdown

locus ListenerL : projection chunked {
    params { port: Int = 8080; }
    accept(c: ConnL) { println("accepted: " + c.peer); }
    run()              { /* accept loop — spawns ConnL per accept */ }
}

locus ConnL {
    params     { fd: Int; peer: String; }
    run()      { /* read/write loop on self.fd */ }
    dissolve() { close(self.fd); }
}

fn main() {
    ListenerL { port: 8080 };
}

A dozen lines. Run it. Press Ctrl-C. Notice what happened, for free:

· SIGINT triggered drain() on the runtime root.
· Drain cascaded depth-first to main's implicit locus.
· That cascaded to its child, the ListenerL.
· ListenerL's drain cascaded to every active ConnL.
· Each ConnL stopped accepting new work, let its in-flight request complete, ran its dissolve() (closing its fd), and freed its arena wholesale.
· ListenerL drained, dissolved, freed its arena.
· Main's locus, with all children gone, dissolved. Process exited 0.

No context.Context threaded through every function. No WaitGroup. No mutex around the connections list. No defer chain. No errgroup coordinating shutdown order. No double-close on a racing connection. No goroutine leak when a handler panics. No "did I get the shutdown order right" because the order falls out of the shape: parent owns child's lifetime; drain cascades depth-first; dissolve frees the region wholesale.

To get the same guarantees in Go, Rust, TypeScript, Python, Java, C#, anything mainstream, you would write — at minimum:

· A signal handler observing SIGINT/SIGTERM.
· A cancellation token / context propagated to every concurrent unit.
· A registry of active connections, protected by a mutex (or a channel-based equivalent).
· A graceful-shutdown coordinator that fans the signal out in dependency order.
· Explicit close() / Drop / __exit__ in each layer, in the correct order.
· A timeout to bound how long to wait for in-flight work.
· A test for the race where shutdown begins as a new connection arrives.
· An ownership model — usually a per-connection allocator or a long-lived pool — so resources can be released without a tracing GC.

Here is what that looks like in practice. Each panel below is an idiomatic implementation of the same shutdown contract — accept connections, track them, on SIGINT close the listener, close every active connection, wait for in-flight work to finish, exit clean: 

package main

import (
    "context"; "log"; "net"
    "os/signal"; "sync"; "syscall"
)

func main() {
    ctx, stop := signal.NotifyContext(context.Background(),
        syscall.SIGINT, syscall.SIGTERM)
    defer stop()

    ln, err := net.Listen("tcp", ":8080")
    if err != nil { log.Fatal(err) }

    var wg sync.WaitGroup
    var mu sync.Mutex
    conns := make(map[net.Conn]struct{})

    go func() {
        <-ctx.Done()
        ln.Close()
        mu.Lock()
        for c := range conns { c.Close() }
        mu.Unlock()
    }()

    for {
        c, err := ln.Accept()
        if err != nil {
            if ctx.Err() != nil { break }
            log.Println(err); continue
        }
        log.Printf("accepted: %s", c.RemoteAddr())
        mu.Lock(); conns[c] = struct{}{}; mu.Unlock()
        wg.Add(1)
        go func(c net.Conn) {
            defer wg.Done()
            defer c.Close()
            defer func() {
                mu.Lock(); delete(conns, c); mu.Unlock()
            }()
            // handle: read/write loop, respect ctx
        }(c)
    }
    wg.Wait()
}

use tokio::io::AsyncWriteExt;
use tokio::net::TcpListener;
use tokio_util::sync::CancellationToken;

#[tokio::main]
async fn main() -> std::io::Result<()> {
    let ln = TcpListener::bind("0.0.0.0:8080").await?;
    let ct = CancellationToken::new();

    let ct_sig = ct.clone();
    tokio::spawn(async move {
        let _ = tokio::signal::ctrl_c().await;
        ct_sig.cancel();
    });

    let mut tasks = tokio::task::JoinSet::new();
    loop {
        tokio::select! {
            _ = ct.cancelled() => break,
            r = ln.accept() => {
                let (mut sock, addr) = r?;
                println!("accepted: {addr}");
                let ct = ct.clone();
                tasks.spawn(async move {
                    tokio::select! {
                        _ = ct.cancelled() => {}
                        // handle: read/write loop on sock
                    }
                    let _ = sock.shutdown().await;
                });
            }
        }
    }
    while tasks.join_next().await.is_some() {}
    Ok(())
}

#include <atomic>
#include <csignal>
#include <iostream>
#include <mutex>

#include <unordered_set>

#include <netinet/in.h>
#include <sys/socket.h>
#include <unistd.h>

static std::atomic<bool> stopping{false};
static std::unordered_set<int> conns;
static std::mutex mu;

static void on_signal(int) {
    stopping = true;
    std::lock_guard lk(mu);
    for (int fd : conns) ::shutdown(fd, SHUT_RDWR);
}

int main() {
    std::signal(SIGINT,  on_signal);
    std::signal(SIGTERM, on_signal);

    int srv = ::socket(AF_INET, SOCK_STREAM, 0);
    int one = 1;
    ::setsockopt(srv, SOL_SOCKET, SO_REUSEADDR, &one, sizeof one);
    sockaddr_in addr{AF_INET, htons(8080), {INADDR_ANY}};
    ::bind(srv, (sockaddr*)&addr, sizeof addr);
    ::listen(srv, 128);

    std::vector<std::jthread> workers;
    while (!stopping) {
        int fd = ::accept(srv, nullptr, nullptr);
        if (fd < 0) { if (stopping) break; continue; }
        std::cout << "accepted: fd=" << fd << "\n";
        { std::lock_guard lk(mu); conns.insert(fd); }
        workers.emplace_back([fd](std::stop_token st) {
            // handle: read/write loop, respect st
            { std::lock_guard lk(mu); conns.erase(fd); }
            ::close(fd);
        });
    }
    ::close(srv);
    // jthread destructor joins all workers
}

import * as net from "net";

async function main() {
  const ac = new AbortController();
  const server = net.createServer();
  const conns = new Set<net.Socket>();
  const tasks: Promise<void>[] = [];

  for (const sig of ["SIGINT", "SIGTERM"] as const) {
    process.once(sig, () => ac.abort());
  }

  server.on("connection", (sock) => {
    const peer = `${sock.remoteAddress}:${sock.remotePort}`;
    console.log(`accepted: ${peer}`);
    conns.add(sock);
    tasks.push(new Promise<void>((resolve) => {
      sock.once("close", () => { conns.delete(sock); resolve(); });
      // handle: read/write on sock events
    }));
  });

  await new Promise<void>((r) => server.listen(8080, r));
  await new Promise<void>((r) =>
    ac.signal.addEventListener("abort", () => r(), { once: true }));

  server.close();
  for (const sock of conns) sock.destroy();
  await Promise.allSettled(tasks);
}

main().catch(console.error);

~ 32 lines · AbortController + Set registry + manual signal wiring

import asyncio
import signal

async def main() -> None:
    handlers: set[asyncio.Task] = set()
    stop = asyncio.Event()

    loop = asyncio.get_running_loop()
    for sig in (signal.SIGINT, signal.SIGTERM):
        loop.add_signal_handler(sig, stop.set)

    async def on_conn(reader, writer):
        peer = writer.get_extra_info("peername")
        print(f"accepted: {peer}")
        handlers.add(asyncio.current_task())
        try:
            # handle: read/write loop, racing stop.wait()
            await stop.wait()
        finally:
            handlers.discard(asyncio.current_task())
            writer.close()
            await writer.wait_closed()

    server = await asyncio.start_server(on_conn, "0.0.0.0", 8080)
    async with server:
        await stop.wait()
    if handlers:
        await asyncio.gather(*handlers, return_exceptions=True)

asyncio.run(main())

~ 30 lines · asyncio + Event + signal handler + task tracking via current_task

Each of those is correct, idiomatic, and load-bearing in production today. Each is between 30 and 45 lines for the bare-minimum shutdown contract — production versions typically grow to 100-300 once keep-alive, TLS, request limits, observability, and proper async-signal-safe signal routing get added. None of it hard, exactly. All of it unenforceable — a refactor that breaks the shutdown order will not fail to compile. It will leak file descriptors in production, on a Tuesday, six weeks from now.

The Aperio version does not get this for free because Aperio is clever. It gets this for free because the shape — parent owns child's lifetime, drain cascades depth-first, dissolve frees the region wholesale — is the language. The compiler does not have to be told. The diagram you would draw on the whiteboard during onboarding is the program.

One annotation in that example deserves a closer look. The : projection chunked on the Listener declares its cost regime — moderate N (typical web load, tens to low hundreds of concurrent connections), with a per-coordinatee sub-region for each accept and a free-list reclaiming bookkeeping slots as connections close. Change it to rich and the same source compiles against a single bump arena (smaller N — say, a few admin sockets — cheaper per accept). Change it to recognition and the same source compiles against a pre-allocated fixed pool (large N — a real-time market data feed of thousands of clients — no allocation in steady state, deterministic capacity). The body never changes. The regime does. One source, three deployments by parameter fit, rather than three near-identical files. No mainstream language offers this; in every other surface, you write the three files.

§ VI  ·  but isn't this just…

Each individual feature has an analog. The shape is the point.

Isn't this just <your familiar language> with extra steps?

Go gets named here most often because its surface is closest to Aperio's, but the same response applies to Rust, Java, Kotlin, C#, Python, Swift, TypeScript, C++. You can build the shape in any of them — goroutines plus channels, threads plus arenas, actors plus mailboxes, async tasks plus supervisors — plus careful package boundaries, plus code review, plus a runbook. People do, every day. What none of them can make load-bearing is the shape itself. Your supervision tree lives in the runbook; the arena hierarchy lives in convention; the N=10 vs N=10 000 distinction lives in three nearly identical files somebody keeps in sync by hand. Every team reinvents the same shape; every onboarding re-derives it from naming and tribal memory. Aperio's bet is that when the shape is the syntax, the compiler enforces what every team currently enforces by standup. The bet is not "Aperio is better than language X." It is no language has yet named the shape.

Isn't a perspective just a Protobuf message?

Protobuf serializes a schema declared separately from producer and consumer. Versions diverge. Wire format becomes a coordination protocol — deprecated fields, "we'll cut over Thursday," the whole rolling-deploy dance. A perspective in Aperio is parameter values within a schema both ends compile from the same source. The schema literally cannot diverge. The bus carries fewer bytes — not because the wire format is cleverer, but because the language already did the work the wire format used to be doing.

Isn't this just OTP?

Yes, on one axis. OTP named the shape of failure flow and let the runtime enforce it. Aperio is what happens when you take that move seriously and apply it to the other three axes simultaneously: lifetime (regions), surface (contracts), and regime (projection class). OTP is the existence proof that this kind of move pays off. Aperio is the bet that the move generalizes.

The diagnostic question — "isn't this just X" — is almost always a sign that the surface did not carry the load-bearing claim. The listener saw a feature; the claim is about a shape. The right pushback, once the shape lands, is not "I can simulate this in Go" — you can — but "I don't trust the compiler to enforce my mental model." That is a real conversation, and the conversation Aperio was built to have.
